# Notable quotes: From one person to 80: Scaling a hypergrowth engineering org with Claude Code

Quotes selected by Claude from the transcript.

> Every new engineer that comes into the company will give him a task to basically use two prompts before he starts working on his task. One, go over all the commits and tell me what everyone is... what everyone can do. Two, go over all the commits and tell me what everyone can do.

*Demonstrates their core philosophy of using simple prompts for onboarding instead of complex documentation processes.*

> The key takeaway I want everyone to get to come out of here, especially for those with small teams, is the fact that you need to keep everything very, very simple.

*Captures the central thesis of their scaling approach emphasizing simplicity over complexity.*

> We assumed a new engineer working on this kind of feature, it requires an integration, it requires working on the agentic flow, it requires a new meta API. We assumed it's going to be a one to two weeks endeavor. And it was really, really awesome to see that we gave that Thursday night, Sunday morning. Everything was ready.

*Provides concrete evidence of the velocity improvements achieved through their Claude-assisted onboarding and development process.*

> A very simple pattern emerged, and that if you look at the conversation, when everything working well, well, the user doesn't say anything. It just goes to the next feature, to the next feature, to the next features. But when things start to break, that's when users get really, really loud inside the chat.

*Illustrates their insight about using user frustration as a quality signal instead of traditional evaluation methods.*

> We can just take, like, the hundred last experiments we had. And we had the matching pull request. And we still had guidelines from that. So we hooked it up to the post-hoc MCP. And had Claude suggest the first iteration of the guidelines.

*Shows how they leveraged historical data and MCP to automate process creation rather than manual committee work.*

> We had to build a user simulator. So when a user types in a request they want an app and some small part of it won't work, that doesn't mean that the evil fails. That was a great epiphany moment for us.

*Captures their key insight about evaluation design focusing on user experience rather than just model output correctness.*

> You can encode a big chunk of your team's, company's taste by looking at your past actions. And that kind of pipes back to the memory talk from the last session where, like, you can just look at what you actually did, like, in the last week or so and understand what your guidelines are.

*Articulates their philosophy of deriving organizational knowledge from historical actions rather than explicit documentation.*

> We really think about, like, bold and simple and sometimes, like, we will take, like, we will work very hard not to build complex things when they're not the right time. Evils is a great example. We hold it off until it was the right moment to build it and then we went all in.

*Demonstrates their strategic approach to timing complex system implementations based on organizational readiness.*
